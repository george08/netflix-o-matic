Humans are watching more Netflix than ever.
We've built an enabler.

At Good, Form & Spectacle we're interested in what happens when you take away search boxes on websites. Turns out it can be pretty fun, especially when you have well-constructed classifications that lead you to movies you didn't know you wanted to watch.

Here's the story of our Netflix Genre Explorer...

December 2, 2013

Alexis Madrigal tweets his curiosity about Netflix "microgenres".

And lists them in a Google doc.

December 5, 2013

Tooling around one evening, I uncover a magic URL, via Mr. Madrigal's sleuthing, with which you can -- if you're a member of Netflix -- bounce around genres semi-randomly just by changing the number part of the URL...

Within moments, Paul Mison and Nick Sweeney to pile on, immediately "considering a dictionary attack" browse.

  • https://gist.github.com/blech/7818890
  • And later: https://gist.github.com/nicksweeney/7852705

January 2, 2014

Madrigal's work ends up in How Netflix Reverse Engineered Hollywood that included a "Netflix genre generator," and lots of interesting stuff about how he pulled everything apart.

"We've now spent several weeks understanding, analyzing, and reverse-engineering how Netflix's vocabulary and grammar work. We've broken down its most popular descriptions, and counted its most popular actors and directors.

"To my (and Netflix's) knowledge, no one outside the company has ever assembled this data before.

"What emerged from the work is this conclusion: Netflix has meticulously analyzed and tagged every movie and TV show imaginable. They possess a stockpile of data about Hollywood entertainment that is absolutely unprecedented. The genres that I scraped and that we caricature above are just the surface manifestation of this deeper database."

So, Paul, Nick and I had been pipped at the post. (We'd though we could maybe build something quick at the time.) I was left feeling that this "caricature" was ultimately just that, and not particularly useful at all in helping people find movies. Could we make something more useful to people wanting something new to watch? My interest is not dismemberment, but revelation and fun.

From then until now, Paul, Nick and I have thought about this when we've had time, set up servers, poking at big spreadsheets, and getting together when we could. Designs started popping out mid-September, and here we are.

We hope you enjoy it! Scramble?
